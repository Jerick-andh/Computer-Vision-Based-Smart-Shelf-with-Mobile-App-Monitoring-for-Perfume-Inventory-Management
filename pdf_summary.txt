1.1​
Project Context 
In recent years, the rapid development of information technology has 
greatly influenced how businesses manage their daily operations. One important 
area affected by this development is inventory management. Traditional inventory 
methods, such as manual counting, spreadsheet recording, and delayed stock 
updates, may still be useful for small businesses, but they can also result in 
inaccurate records, limited monitoring, and difficulty in tracking products across 
different locations. As retail businesses continue to expand through physical stores 
and online platforms, there is a growing need for automated systems that can 
provide more accurate, faster, and more reliable inventory monitoring. 
In the Philippines, many small and medium-sized retail businesses still rely 
on manual or semi-manual inventory management due to limited resources and the 
cost of adopting advanced technologies. These businesses commonly use 
logbooks, spreadsheets, or basic digital records to monitor product availability. 
Although these methods are simple and affordable, they are prone to human error, 
delayed updates, and stock discrepancies. For businesses with more than one

At the local level, perfume retailers and resellers also experience similar 
inventory management challenges. Perfume products are usually stored in bottles 
with different scents, sizes, and categories, making manual tracking more difficult 
as the number of products increases. Aside from inventory accuracy, proper 
storage conditions are also important because perfumes may be affected by 
unsuitable temperature conditions. If the storage area becomes too warm, the 
quality, scent stability, and shelf life of the perfume may be affected. This 
highlights the need for a system that can monitor not only product quantity but 
also the environmental condition inside the shelf. 
Otto Scents, a perfume business primarily operating in San Pablo, Laguna 
with a branch in Lipa City, Batangas, currently manages its inventory using 
spreadsheet-based records. This process may lead to inaccurate stock tracking, 
delayed updates, and difficulty in monitoring the actual number of perfume bottles 
available in each branch. The absence of an automated monitoring system limits 
the ability of the owner and staff to verify shelf inventory in real time. In addition, 
traditional and manual inventory management processes are often labor-intensive

and prone to errors, which may result in inventory discrepancies, inefficient stock

To address these concerns, this study proposes the development of a 
Computer Vision-Based Smart Shelf with Mobile App Monitoring for Perfume 
Inventory Management. The system will use a Raspberry Pi paired with a camera 
module to monitor perfume bottles placed inside the smart shelf. During normal 
operation, the Raspberry Pi will process captured shelf images locally using a 
YOLO-based object detection model. The detected perfume bottles will then be 
mapped to predefined Regions of Interest, where each region represents a specific 
shelf section or perfume category. Through this approach, the system will detect 
bottle presence, monitor stock movement, count detected bottles per shelf region, 
and compare the detected quantity with the inventory records. If the Raspberry Pi 
fails to process YOLO detection due to model error, timeout, low confidence, or 
device limitation, the captured image will be sent to Google Cloud for fallback 
processing. Computer vision-based inventory systems have been shown to 
significantly improve accuracy, reduce manual labor, and enhance monitoring 
capabilities in retail environments (Hassan et al., 2025).

3.5 Development 
The development of the proposed system will be carried out through 
iterative Agile cycles. Hardware and software components will be developed in 
parallel and integrated gradually. During hardware development, the Raspberry Pi, 
camera module or USB camera, temperature sensor, cooling fan, relay module or 
MOSFET module, LED indicators, power components, and network connection 
will be configured and tested. The Raspberry Pi program will be responsible for

camera input handling, YOLO-based object detection, Region of Interest mapping, 
temperature acquisition, cooling fan control, LED status indication, event logging, 
local storage during connection interruptions, and Google Cloud Firestore 
synchronization. During normal operation, the Raspberry Pi will process captured 
shelf images locally using YOLO. If local YOLO detection fails, times out, or 
produces low-confidence results, the captured image may be sent to Google Cloud 
for fallback processing. The Android application will be developed to receive and 
display system data for the owner, admin, and branch staff. Integration testing will 
then be conducted to confirm successful communication among the Raspberry Pi, 
Google Cloud Firestore, cloud failover service, and the mobile application.
